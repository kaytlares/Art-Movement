History of the Baroque Art Movement

The term baroque is coined from the Portuguese word barroco, which means imperfect-shaped pearl in Portuguese. This art movement thrived throughout 17th century Europe. The push behind the art movement was to counteract the protestant reformation. When Martin Luther wrote his 95 theses, acting against the Roman Catholic church, the churches had to respond to the backlash they were facing. With a lot of people lacking the ability to read, the church decided to tell their stories through art, rather than through words. Once the churches responded to this matter, the ravishing baroque art movement was born. Even though art is somewhat static, the movement sought to bring art alive, with a feel for the dramatics. The artwork makes it feel as if the viewer is watching the story unfold right in front of them. And this didn't just take place in art, but the baroque movement also dabbled in architecture, sculptures, and music. This movement is definitely something different than what all the other movements have. It didn't focus on the ideals of perfect people, like the Greeks. Instead, it used normal day people, as a medium for its movement. The movement also played very heavily on a technique called churrasco. Which is a strong contrast between light and dark colors. From this quirky movement, we have painters and architects like Caravaggio, and Francisco Bernini. Furthermore, we also have musicians like Johann Sebastian Bach, Antonio Vivaldi and George Frideric Handel.
